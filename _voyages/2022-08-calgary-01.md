This is part 1 of a series of articles I wrote to preserve a memory of my week-long trip to Banff and Calgary in August 2022. It’s a mix of anecdotes, relevant or not, with some information here and there that you might find interesting if you’re planning to go too.

## Day 0 — Friday, Aug. 26th
### 4 PM EDT
I left my apartment at 3:15 to wait for the 747 bus that goes from downtown Montréal to the airport. The 3:50 bus didn’t show up, so I had time to talk to an Italian couple in their late 50s who had been waiting longer than me. Coincidentally, they had been in Banff a few days prior and were on their way to YUL to return to Europe. I asked them what they enjoyed doing in Banff. They told me that they had rented a canoe from the Banff Canoe Club and were able to get to Vermillion Lake via Echo Creek since the water level was high enough for them to navigate over a beaver dam that normally blocks access to the lake. I wrote it down, just in case.

### 6:30 PM EDT
I passed the security check, a 200ml jar of sunscreen lighter and I’m now waiting for my flight. It was delayed by an hour because the previous flight at the gate is late. Gate 17 is crowded with people, some going to YVR, others, like me, to YYC. I tell my Airbnb host, who is expecting me at 11:15 PM MDT, hoping that the delay will not make me arrive later than midnight at his place.

### 11:55 PM MDT
The plane landed at YYC at 11:30 PM. During the entire flight, the guy in seat 13B (I was in 14D) threw up and/or made vomiting noises. I guess 13 wasn’t his lucky row. I was a little upset with him because it prevented me from having a good sleep on the plane, but at the same time, I figured that having been sick for 4.5 hours, he probably had a lousier flight than I did.

I’m glad I only brought a carry-on suitcase; I can avoid the carousel and go directly to the cabs. About 20 minutes and $45 later, I arrive at my Airbnb, located in the Bridgeland neighborhood. It’s a nice 3-story townhouse. The guy is friendly, shows me to my room on the top floor, tells me there are muffins and bananas I can have for breakfast, and I head off to bed.

## Day 1 — Saturday, Aug. 27th
### 9 AM MDT
With a comfortable bed and silence instead of an airplane seat and vomiting noises, I slept very well. I do a little digging on the Internet to see what I could do and where I could eat in Calgary while waiting for my 2:45 PM bus to Banff. The house is quiet. I carefully make my way downstairs so as to not wake up my host and the other guests. I grab a banana and a muffin and without further ado, I leave the house to go get breakfast at Red’s Diner in the Inglewood/Ramsay neighborhood.

![The city, as seen from Bridgeland](/docs/assets/202208_alberta/20220910-calgary-1-bridgeland.png)

With a carry-on suitcase to lug around, it’s about a 45-minute walk. My first impression of Calgary: people are very friendly. Or maybe it’s just a Saturday morning. One lady in a car asked me if I was lost and even got out of her car to give me directions. Thankfully, because with no access to the Internet, I would’ve had a hard time finding the small path that leads from 12th St. to 11th St by myself.

![In case you’re also looking for it at some point, this is the path that leads from 12th St. SE to 11th St. SE](/docs/assets/202208_alberta/20220910-calgary-1-12th.png)

### 11:30 AM MDT
After a traditional but satisfying breakfast at Red’s Diner, I took advantage of being in Inglewood to go hat shopping at Smithbilt Hats, a century-old Calgary institution. Very friendly staff. The girl at the counter is pretty and I was able to exchange a few words in French with Max, the employee who helped me customize my hat. He seemed proud when I showed him that Smithbilt Hats is mentioned several times in my travel guide. My hat cost me $178 (including taxes), but I’m going home with an authentic, made-in-Calgary souvenir that will protect me from the sun during the long days I’ll spend outside in Banff.

![Smithbilt Hats](/docs/assets/202208_alberta/20220910-calgary-1-smithbilt-hats.png)

### 2:45 PM MDT
With my new hat on and still carrying that annoying suitcase, I walked downtown to be close to where I had to wait for my bus to Banff.

Calgary is very easy to navigate. As it is in New York, streets (North-South) and avenues (East-West) are numbered and suffixed with “NE”, “NW”, “SW” or “SE” depending on which quadrant of the city they are in. The Bow River separates the North from the South and Centre St. separates the East from the West.

![In Downtown Calgary](/docs/assets/202208_alberta/20220910-calgary-1-city.png)

From Inglewood, I made my way downtown via 9th Ave. SE. I walked a bit on the pedestrian Stephen Avenue (8th Ave. SW). I try not to stray too far from the Calgary Tower, near which I have to catch the On-It bus that will take me to Banff. There seems to be something going on at the Olympic Plaza; I decide to go have a look.
It turns out to be the Red Bull Outliers 2022, a motocross competition with obstacles. It’s crowded and I don’t have a good view of the action, but it allows me to settle down with my suitcase. The two play-by-play commentators of the event remind me of John & John, who hosted the original version of Wipeout USA. The reference is old, but the point is that in my whole life, this is definitely the hour where I’ve heard “eats a concrete sandwich” the most.

![Red Bull Outliers 2022 at the Olympic Plaza](/docs/assets/202208_alberta/20220910-calgary-1-redbull-outliers.png)

Dark clouds move in quickly and it is under a heavy downpour that I get to the pick-up location for my bus to Banff. Fortunately, it is already there waiting and I don’t have to stay under the shower.
